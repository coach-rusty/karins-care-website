5 Things a Professional Housekeeper Notices the Moment She Walks In

By Karin Eagle Smith  ·  Surprise, AZ  ·  25 Years Experience

After 25 years walking into people's homes across the Surprise and West Valley area, I've developed what my clients jokingly call "housekeeper's eyes." I see things most homeowners have genuinely stopped seeing — not because they don't care, but because it happens to all of us. We stop noticing our own spaces. Here's what I notice within the first two minutes of arriving.

1. The Entryway Floor

The entry tells me everything. Is there a buildup of grit along the baseboards? Are shoes scattered or lined up? The entryway is where outside comes inside, and in the Arizona desert, that means dust, pollen, and fine gravel tracking in constantly. If the entry floor looks neglected, I know the rest of the home has usually followed suit.

A quick weekly sweep and a good doormat on both sides of the front door makes an enormous difference. It's the cheapest thing you can do to keep your whole home cleaner.

2. The Kitchen Sink

Not the counters — the sink. Most people clean around the sink but not the sink itself. The drain edges, the corners where the sink meets the counter, the faucet base where water pools and mineral deposits form. In our hard-water West Valley area, that buildup happens fast. Left alone, that's where mildew starts.

I scrub the sink first on every visit. It sets the tone for the whole kitchen, and a clean sink makes every surrounding surface look better instantly.

3. Baseboards and Floor Edges

Vacuums and mops hit the middle of the floor. The edges — where the baseboard meets the floor — collect dust, pet hair, and grime that gets pushed to the side but never actually picked up. After months of this, there's a visible line of buildup that makes even a cleaned room feel grimy.

I work the edges every visit. Your room will feel cleaner immediately, even if you can't quite explain why. It's the difference between "swept" and actually clean.

4. Ceiling Fans

I always look up. Ceiling fans in Arizona run almost year-round — you know this — and the dust they collect gets redistributed into the air every time the blades spin. A thick ridge of dust on top of each blade is something most homeowners only discover when they're already sneezing, or when a guest happens to glance up.

Clean your ceiling fan blades monthly. It takes five minutes and the air quality difference is real.

5. Behind and Under Things

The back of the toilet. Under the bathroom vanity. Behind the refrigerator. The spaces we move things away from to clean — and then put back without actually cleaning underneath. I pull things out. Not every single visit, but regularly and on a rotation. That's what keeps a home from accumulating the kind of buildup that only reveals itself when you're moving furniture.

This is the area most DIY cleaning misses entirely, and it's the area that most defines whether a home feels genuinely clean or just tidied.
