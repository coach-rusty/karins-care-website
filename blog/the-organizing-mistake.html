The One Organizing Mistake Almost Every Homeowner Makes

By Karin Eagle Smith  ·  Surprise, AZ  ·  25 Years Experience

I've organized a lot of homes over 25 years. Guest rooms full of boxes that haven't been opened since the last move. Garages with no visible floor. Pantries with expired items from years ago. Closets where the rod had actually given up and collapsed. And in almost every case, the homeowner had tried to do it themselves — multiple times — and it kept coming undone. Here's why.

The Mistake: Organizing Things You Should Have Let Go

Most people start organizing by buying bins, baskets, and drawer dividers. They sort things into neat piles, find homes for everything, label what they can reach, and feel genuinely good about it. Then six months later, it's chaos again.

The reason this keeps happening isn't lack of effort or willpower. It's that you can't organize clutter. You can only move it around.

The first step isn't sorting. It isn't buying containers. It's editing — deciding what actually belongs in your home before a single bin is purchased or a single shelf is labeled.

The Three-Question Test

Before I touch a single item in a space I'm organizing, I walk through it with my client and we ask three questions about everything:

For each item, ask yourself:

  1. Do I use this regularly? Not "could I use this someday" — do I actually use it.
  2. Do I love it? Not "it was expensive" or "it was a gift" — do I genuinely want it in my home.
  3. If I needed it and couldn't find it, would I actually notice it was gone?

If the answer to all three is no — it leaves the house. Donate, give to someone who will use it, or let it go. This is the step most people rush past or skip entirely, and it's the reason organizing never sticks.

What Organizing Actually Is

Once you've edited — and this step genuinely takes longer than most people want it to — then you organize. Now you're giving permanent, logical homes to things that deserve to be in your home. Now the bins make sense. Now the labels stick. Now you can put something back in the right place because there's a right place that makes sense to you.

Organization isn't about having the right containers. It's about having the right things.

The Arizona Storage Factor

In the West Valley, many of my clients deal with a specific challenge: seasonal clutter. Holiday decorations. Pool equipment that's needed eight months a year and stored four. Monsoon gear. The winter clothing that doesn't get used much but still needs somewhere to live.

These items end up jumbled together in garages and storage rooms and create a chaos that makes everything else harder to find and manage. My recommendation: dedicate clearly zoned, labeled areas by season and category. Pool equipment in one zone. Holiday by holiday in their own boxes. Winter and summer clothing separated and clearly identified.

The goal isn't a garage that looks like a magazine photo. It's a garage where you can find anything you need in under two minutes without moving four other things to get to it.

Start Small, Start Once

Pick one space. Not the whole house — one drawer, one closet, one cabinet. Do the edit first. Remove everything. Ask the three questions. Then put back only what passed. Then organize what's left.

That's it. Do it once, do it right, and that space will stay organized because there's finally room for everything that's in it.
